1. Our Advanced Technology

We use autologous adult adipose-derived stem cells for deployment and clinical research. Early stem cell research has traditionally been associated with the controversial use of “embryonic” stem cells. The new focus is on non-embryonic, non-re-engineered, and not genetically modified autologous adult mesenchymal stem cells, which are found in a person’s own blood, bone marrow, and fat. Cell therapy around the world is shifting its focus from bone marrow-based cells to adipose tissue (fat) derived cells since the cells are easy to obtain and generally very robust.

Adipose fat is an abundant and reliable source of stem cells. The best quality adipose cells are derived from the enzymatic digestion of liposuctioned fat, which can be performed at the bedside in an essentially closed system to protect sterility. Autologous stem cells from a person’s own fat are easy to harvest safely under local anesthesia and are abundant and rich in quantities up to 2500 times those seen in bone marrow.

Clinical success and favorable outcomes appear to be related directly to the quantity of stem cells deployed. Once these adipose-derived stem cells are administered back into the patient, they have the potential to repair human tissue by influencing healing and also forming new cells of mesenchymal origin, such as cartilage, bone, ligaments, tendons, nerve, fat, muscle, blood vessels, and certain internal organs. Stem cells’ ability to form cartilage and bone makes them potentially highly effective in the treatment of degenerative orthopedic conditions. Their ability to form new blood vessels and other tissues makes them suitable for mitigating a large number of traumatic and degenerative conditions.

We have anecdotal and clinical-experimental evidence that autologous stem cell therapy is effective in healing and regeneration. Stem cells seek out damaged tissues to repair the body naturally. The solid literature and the Internet are full of successful reports and testimonials, and there are definitive studies demonstrating the safety and efficacy of stem cell therapy. Such data took five to ten years to accumulate. In an effort to provide relief for patients suffering from certain degenerative and chronic diseases that have been resistant to common modalities of treatment. Initially, we conducted pilot studies as experimental tests of treatment effectiveness using very high numbers of adipose-derived stem cells obtained from fat.

Our techniques for cell harvesting and isolation utilize advanced technologies. This breakthrough enables patients to safely receive their own autologous stem cells in large quantities. Our treatments and research are funded by associates and patients, and we have successfully worked to make them affordable. All our procedures are sterile, non-invasive, and performed under local anesthesia. Patients looking for non-surgical alternatives for degenerative disorders can benefit from our cellular treatments by first completing the “Applicant's Quick Assessment” form to determine their eligibility. We take pride in being at the forefront of the emerging field of Regenerative Medicine, and cell research and treatment.

2. What is Regenerative Medicine?

Regenerative medicine is a cutting-edge medical field focused on repairing, replacing, or regenerating damaged human cells, tissues, and organs to restore their normal function, often by harnessing the body's own healing abilities or growing new tissues in the lab, using approaches such as stem cells, gene therapy, and biologics. It aims to treat root causes of diseases and injuries, offering potential cures rather than just managing symptoms, with applications in orthopedics, chronic diseases, and organ failure by stimulating previously irreparable damaged organs to heal themselves. Researchers working in the field of regenerative medicine have already made amazing progress in trying to compensate for failing organs. What if we could readily replace diseased?. (www.nih.gov)

3. What are the tools of Regenerative Medicine?

Traditionally, we have used various medications and hormones to limit disease and help the body repair itself. For example, hormone replacement therapy has, in many cases, shown the ability to more optimally help the immune system and thus help us repair diseased or injured tissues. Genetic research is an evolving area where we will eventually learn and utilize more ways of specifically dealing with gene defects causing degenerative disease. Stem cell therapy has already shown considerable promise in treating many degenerative conditions. Stem cells communicate with our tissues and with each other through a complex system of protein signaling molecules called cytokines or “growth factors.” There are thousands of growth factors, and some FDA-approved and clinically available, such as AQ (brand) topical growth factors, a brand using patented technology to deliver human growth factors (GFs) and cytokines topically to stimulate the skin's natural healing and regeneration, which are useful for several cosmetic and therapeutic purposes. The science of using growth factors with stem cells has evolved and represents the next main area of growth in regenerative medicine.

4. What is a Stem Cell?

A stem cell is basically any cell that can replicate and differentiate. This means the cell can not only multiply, it can turn into different types of tissues. There are different kinds of stem cells. Most people are familiar with or have heard the term “embryonic stem cell”. These are cells from the embryonic stage that have yet to differentiate – as such, they can change into any body part at all. These are then called “Pluripotent” cells. Because they are taken from unborn or unwanted embryos, there has been considerable controversy surrounding their use. Also, while they have been used in some areas of medicine – particularly, outside the United States – they have also been associated with occasional tumor (Teratoma) formations. There is a work being conducted by several companies to isolate particular lines of embryonic stem cells for future use.

Another kind of stem cell is the “adult stem cell.” This is a stem cell that already resides in one’s body within different tissues. In recent times, much work has been done isolating bone-marrow derived stem cells. These are also known as “mesenchymal stem cells” because they come from the mesodermal section of your body. They can differentiate into bone and cartilage, and probably all other mesodermal elements, such as fat, connective tissue, blood vessels, muscle and nerve tissue. Bone marrow stem cells can be extracted and because they are low in numbers, they are usually cultured in order to multiply their numbers for the next use.

As it turns out, fat is also loaded with mesenchymal stem cells. In fact, it has hundreds if not thousands of times more stem cells compared to bone marrow. Today, we actually have tools that allow us to separate the stem cells from fat. Because most people have adequate fat supplies and the numbers of stem cells are so great, there is no need to culture the cells over a period of days, and they can be used right away.

5. How do adult stem cells heal?

These adult stem cells are known as “progenitor” cells. This means they remain dormant (do nothing) unless they witness some level of tissue injury. It’s the tissue injury that turns them on. So, when a person has a degenerative type problem, the stem cells tend to go to that area of need and stimulate the healing process. Suffice it to say that there are multiple animal models and a plethora of human evidence that indicates these are significant reparative cells.

7. Is our procedure FDA-approved?

Our surgical procedures are considered a “physician's practice of medicine”, which falls outside the jurisdiction of the FDA, allowing the physician and patient to freely determine their course of treatment. The FDA does have guidelines about treatment and manipulation of a patient’s own tissues. At our global network we meet these guidelines by providing same day treatment with the patient’s own cells that undergo no manipulation and are inserted during the same procedure. The US FDA issued an exemption for the use of autologous adult stem cells for the treatment of any specific disease in the United States.

FDA Section 21 CFR 1271.15(b) Provides:
“You are not required to comply with the requirements of this Part if you are an establishment that removes “Human Cells, Tissues, and Cellular and Tissue-Based Products (HCT/Ps)" from an individual (autologous use) and implants such HCT/Ps into the same individual during the same surgical procedure, and the HCT/Ps remain in their original form.”.

Another “Exemption” from the European Parliament Tissue Directive 2004/23/EC:
“(8) This Directive excludes blood and blood products (other than haematopoietic progenitor cells) and human organs, as well as organs, tissues, or cells of animal origin. Blood and blood products are currently regulated by Directives 2001/83/EC and 2000/70/EC, Recommendation 98/463/EC and Directive 2002/98/EC.
Tissues and cells used as an autologous graft (tissues removed and transplanted back to the same individual), within the same surgical procedure and without being subjected to any banking process, are also excluded from this Directive.”.

8. Do we use any embryonic stem cells?

No. Only adult mesenchymal stem cells are used. These cells are capable of forming bone, cartilage, fat, muscle, ligaments, blood vessels, and certain organs. Embryonic stem cells are associated with ethical considerations and limitations.

9. Non Embryonic Adult Stem Cells

Adult (not from embryonic source) Mesenchymal Stem Cells are undifferentiated cells that have the ability to replace dying cells and regenerate damaged tissue. These special cells seek out areas of injury, disease and destruction where they are capable of regenerating healthy cells and enabling a person’s natural healing processes to be accelerated. As we gain a deeper understanding of their medical function and apply this knowledge, we are realizing their enormous therapeutic potential to help the body heal itself. Adult stem cells have been used for a variety of medical treatments to repair and regenerate acute and chronically damaged tissues in humans and animals (racing horses/ camels, farm livestock, and home pets). Although adult stem cells are found in many organs, they are very accessible in high numbers from fat tissue. Other sources of stem cells such as “amniotic” products which are commercially available are not from one’s own body, and these cells are gamma-radiated by the manufacturer and, therefore, are not live cells. If you have been offered amniotic cells from some source, one should be aware of this fact.

Many reputable international centers have been using stem cell therapy to treat various chronic degenerative conditions as diverse as severe neurologic diseases, renal failure, erectile dysfunction, degenerative orthopedic problems, and even cardiac and pulmonary diseases, to name a few. Adult stem cells appear to be particularly effective at repairing cartilage in degenerated joints.

13. Once a candidate is selected for treatment, how do we obtain a person’s stem cells?

Autologous adult stem cells are harvested and deployed during the same procedure. Patients undergo a minimally invasive liposuction-type harvesting procedure by a certified specialized surgeon in our network. The harvesting procedure generally lasts a few minutes and can be done under local anesthesia. SVF Stromal Vascular Fraction, with its mesenchymal stem cells and growth factors is then processed from about 2 ounces (approx. 57 grams) of fat using a closed sterile system to avoid contact with the environment. SVF is ready for deployment within 90 minutes or less.

14. Why do we use liposuction Fat rather than Bone Marrow as a source of stem cells?

Bone marrow sampling (a somewhat uncomfortable procedure) typically yields around 5,000 to 60,000 cells. These cells are cultured over several days to produce a few million cells before they are injected into the patient.

Recent advancements in stem cell science have enabled the extraction of large quantities of high-quality multipotent cells from a person’s own liposuction fat. Using advanced technology, doctors can process this fat to obtain approximately 500,000 to 1 million stem cells per cubic centimeter.

Consequently, it is possible to harvest as many as 10 million to 40 million cells (or more) from a single treatment. These adipose-derived stem cells can differentiate into various cell types when deployed correctly, including bone, cartilage, tendon (connective tissue), muscle, blood vessels, and nerve tissue, among others.

15. How is the Fat obtained?

Patients have their fat (usually abdominal) harvested using our special sterile harvesting technology under a local anesthetic. The “mini liposuction” fat removal procedure lasts approximately twenty minutes. Specially designed equipment is used to harvest the fat cells, and less than 100cc of fat is required. Post-operative discomfort is minimal, and there is minimal restriction on activity.

16. How do we control sterility in the processing of the stem cells?

Stem cells are harvested under sterile conditions using a special “closed system” technology so that the cells never come into contact with the environment throughout the entire process from removal to deployment. Sterile technique and oral antibiotics are also used to prevent infection.

17. Do we use stem cells from any other animal source or person?

No. Only a person’s own adult autologous cells are used. These are harvested from each individual and deployed back into their own body. There is no risk of contamination of the treatments.

18. Why do some stem cell facilities around the world require days to weeks before removal of mesenchymal stem cells until reinsertion into the patient?

These facilities are obtaining stem cells from bone marrow or blood in relatively small quantities, and they are then culturing (growing) the cells to create adequate quantities. Research seems to indicate that the success of treatment is directly related to the quantity of cells injected. We use adipose-derived stem cells that are abundant naturally at approximately 2,500 times the levels found in bone marrow (the most common source of mesenchymal stem cells). Our medical network centers use technology that isolates adipose stem cells in vast numbers in a short time span so that prolonged culturing is unnecessary, and cells can be deployed into a patient within 90 minutes of harvesting.

21. Can stem cells treat cancer?

Stem cells have played an important role in cancer therapy for five decades. Hematopoietic stem cells (similar to mesenchymal stem cells and also found in stromal vascular fraction) have been used to restore bone marrow and replace blood cell lines after planned ablation of a cancer patient’s bone marrow. This is known as a “stem cell transplant” and is associated with significant risk to cancer patients. Of course, this is different than a regenerative therapy using stem cells to replace or repair damaged tissue.

Having said that, new advances in cancer therapy have exploited the stem cell property known as “TROPISM,” or organotropism, which is the ability of stem cells to target tumors. Stem cell tropism—the ability to migrate to tumors—improves treatment by delivering therapeutic agents directly to cancer sites, while clinical trials utilize modified stem cells to secrete agents like TRAIL and interferon-beta (IFN-β).

This property has been used to help carry cancer-killing agents deep into the cancer, where the body’s immune system cannot always operate effectively. We are involved in cutting edge clinical trials using stem cells to carry cancer-killing biologic agents deep into cancer tissue that has not responded to conventional therapy.

22. Can stem cells cause cancer?

Adult mesenchymal stem cells are not known to cause cancer. Some patients have heard of stories of cancer caused by stem cells, but these are probably related to the use of embryonic cells (Not Adult Mesenchymal Cells). These embryonic tumors, known as "teratomas", are rare but possible occurrences when embryonic cells are used, which are cells that do not know where (or when) to stop dividing, multiplying, and differentiating.

23. What about Patients with Known Cancers?

Stem cell therapy is thought to be safe and not affect dormant cancers. If someone has had cancer that was treated and responded successfully, there is no reason to withhold stem cell deployment. In most cases, stem cells should not be used in patients with known active cancer outside of an active protocol (see “21. Can Stem Cells Treat Cancer?”).

26. Are there any biological agents added to the stem cells, such as medications or mixtures containing plasma-enriched platelets?

Our adipose-derived stem cell harvesting and isolation technique yields extremely high numbers of stem cells. In reviewing outcomes data, treatment cell numbers appear to correlate with treatment success. Our cells are actually in a type of “soup” called Stromal Vascular Fraction SVF which is stem cells bathed in a rich mixture of natural growth factors (Not the same as human growth factor hormone, which is only one type of growth factor). Some types of orthopedic and urologic diseases appear to respond better to stem cells that are super-enriched with growth factors created by administering Platelet Rich Plasma to the patient. Autologous Platelet Rich Plasma is derived from a patient’s own blood drawn at the time of deployment. We do not add any foreign substances or medications to the stem cells.

27. How are the cells deployed into a patient?

Stromal Vascular Fraction (SVF) cells, derived from a patient's own fat tissue via liposuction, are typically deployed through local injections directly into damaged tissues (joints, organs) or systemically via intravenous (IV) infusion. This same-day procedure often combines both methods to treat inflammatory and degenerative conditions, with cells usually administered within 90 minutes of processing.

Methods of Deployment:
Intravenous (IV) Infusion: The concentrated SVF solution is injected into a vein, typically delivering 150 to 200 million cells for systemic, body-wide effects.
Local Injection: The SVF is injected directly into a specific, targeted area, such as a joint (intra-articular), near a joint (para-articular), or around specific tissues (e.g., retrobulbar) to promote localized healing.
Combined Approach: Often, a portion of the cells is administered intravenously, while the remainder is injected directly into the affected, inflamed area.

Biomaterial Harvesting: Adipose tissue is removed via a minimally invasive, local anesthesia liposuction.
Processing: The fat is processed (enzymatic digestion, centrifugation) to isolate the SVF.
Deployment: The resulting cellular mixture is injected into the patient immediately after preparation.

The entire procedure usually takes about 4 hours, and patients are often advised to limit activity for about a week following the injection to allow for cell attachment.

Different conditions are treated in different ways, and there are different degrees of success. FDA regulations prevent us from making any claims about expectations for success; however, if you are chosen for treatment, it will be explained that we believe stem cell therapy may be beneficial, or in some cases, that we are unsure, and treatment would be considered investigational.

Stem cell therapy relies on the body’s own regenerative healing to occur. The regenerative process may take time, particularly with orthopedic patients, who may not see results for several months. In some diseases, more immediate responses are possible. Stem cell therapy will scarcely help heavy-smoking patients, psychiatric conditions, or those with HIV, or hepatitis B or C. Unfortunately, many greedy commercial medical clinics will not disclose this fact to their patients to save them the effort and costs. They advocate misleading information and ads.

Your best protection against clinics promoting unproven/ fraudulent stem cell treatments is understanding the science behind your illness, injury, or condition. Follow our educational and public awareness seminars, workshops, gatherings, and conferences.

36. Asking for a Second Opinion for a Cell Therapy

A second opinion involves having the specialist(s) at the “Stem Cell Therapy Center” review your medical history after your consultation with your doctor. It can help you feel more confident in your other optional treatment or gain greater clarity about your diagnosis. Your doctor should be supportive and help you in the process of getting a second opinion. Once the specialist(s) has evaluated your medical history, they may recommend additional tests at the center (or any hospital).

A second opinion is not a transfer: Treatment will still be provided by your doctor, even during your second opinion, although you can discuss a transfer to the Center with your physician during your second opinion. If you are invited to a consultation with one of the specialists, the appointment will take place at the Center or through a telephonic or video call. Sometimes you are not invited for a consultation appointment. In that case, the Center shall inform you about the reasons behind that decision. Please note that we always review your medical files thoroughly and confidentially before making this decision.

Transfer of treatment: Requesting a transfer of treatment means that you will receive treatment at the "Stem Cell Therapy Center" instead of your current doctor. Once the transfer has been processed, we will discuss and implement your treatment plan. Upon your request for a transfer, we will review your medical history to see whether we are able to take you on as a patient. The Center takes all necessary expertise, the availability, and the necessary time frame of your treatment into consideration during this process. You will keep contact with your current doctor until your request has been approved. You can request a transfer to the center at any time.

The Cost: Most health insurance providers will cover one second opinion a year. Please make sure to ask your provider whether your tests, treatments, or second opinion will be covered. If your health insurance does not cover the costs, you will be requested to pay an advance fee. You can pay before your appointment. The actual costs will be settled afterwards. These depend on the care provided, such as additional testing.

In case you would like to continue your treatment in the Center. If your costs are not covered by your (national) health insurance, a care administration officer at the Center will notify you of the expected costs beforehand.

39. Stem Cell Banking

Cryo-preservation (Cryobanking in liquid nitrogen LN2 vapor) of adipose-derived stem cells is possible and can be performed at the same time as the patient's “Stromal Vascular Fraction” SVF deployment procedure. Autologous lipo-aspirate can be frozen as SVF (contains mesenchymal and hematopoietic stem cells). SVF is a mixture of regenerative cells (such as stem cells) and growth factors derived from your own fat tissue, used in regenerative medicine for healing and anti-aging conditions. SVF can be deployed for repeated treatments and also expanded protocols under IRB (Institutional Review Board) and the regulatory bodies' approval as part of a safety trial, providing vast quantities of autologous adult stem cells that could be used throughout that patient’s life.

This will provide patients with their own personal stem cell line that could be used “on demand” for everything from treating stroke, heart attack, serious accident, or even cancer therapy should the need arise. The Cells preservation program is also vital since many of the conditions we treat such as neurodegenerative disorders and auto-immune conditions often require repeat deployments and using banked autologous cells allows this to be done in a convenient and cost effective manner.

40. Who sets the regulations?

Various official regulatory bodies around the world provide guidelines for the US Current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMP). These guidelines focus on five elements: people, premises, processes, products, and procedures. For example, in Europe, the European Union, in consultation with relevant authorities in member states and the European Medicines Agency (EMA), develops legislation and subsequently provides guidelines, while the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) is responsible for this in the United States. Many other countries also adopt cGMP guidelines from the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the World Health Organization (WHO).
